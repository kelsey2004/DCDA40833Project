Historical Context: Introduction to Media Empires

"You need to control everything. The game's over when they break you up. But in the meantime you play to win. And you know you've won when the government stops you." - Ted Turner.

Around the 1980s the businesses of film, broadcast, and cable were largely separate and distinct. The studios, television networks, and cable companies were legally, strategically, and structurally disconnected. Regulatory constraints such as The Sherman Antitrust Act ensured that vertical integration and horizontal integration were pursued only with extreme caution, and in small measure.

What is Anti-trust Regulation?
  • Antitrust laws in general are designed to promote fair competition and prevent monopolies.
  • Antitrust regulation formally began in the nineteenth century.
  • The legislation was a product of the Industrial Revolution and the public outcry over the burgeoning "trusts" dominating industry at the time. It was designed to combat the chokehold on business resulting from monopolistic practices.

During the 1980s, President Reagan was especially intent on eliminating the use of antitrust enforcement as a means of regulating business, thereby helping the film, broadcast, and cable industries to begin their path toward structural convergence. It was during the Reagan presidency that his rollback of media ownership rules opened the door for unprecedented consolidation (1980-1996). The entertainment industry's rich and complex history with antitrust issues dates back to the earliest days of the silent film era, when the courts dismantled Edison's Motion Picture Patents Company (MPPC). Broadcast Networks entered an era of competition with cable. Early 20th-century newspaper chains, radio networks, and film studios all expanded by buying competitors and vertically integrating.

  • Rupert Murdoch's News Corporation bought Twentieth Century-Fox in 1985
  • Sony purchased Columbia and Tri-Star in 1989
  • MGM was taken over by Pathé in 1990
  • Matsushita bought MCA Universal the same year.

Netflix as Innovation

In 2007, Netflix's innovation turned the Entertainment Industry upside-down and placed streaming content, movies, and television shows, into the hands of anyone with a mere flat and small monthly subscription. Anyone, anywhere around the world can watch movies and television programming in the comfort of their own home, on any device like a smartphone or laptop, without a set time. Netflix has accuired 200 million subscribers in more than 190 countries, making it the largest subscription video on-demand platform in the world

But what has made this possible?

Infrastructure and Interface

Netflix has their own team of "taggers" who are in charge of classifying every single title on the platform with relevant and efficient descriptors.

  • Tags include: tone, pacing, romance level, violence, sexual content, plot conclusiveness, and more.

These tags are combined with user behavior tracking. Netflix knows what you watched, when you watched it, where you pause, where you stopped, when you log on, how long you log on, the genres you like… it's all being collected. Netflix's extensive data of user behavior has allowed for personalization through its algorithm-based recommendation that tailors content to you. No two user's homepage is alike, making them stand out from their competitors.

Just as earlier media empires used mergers and acquisitions to consolidate power, Netflix uses data, algorithms, and global infrastructure to understand their audience and dominate markets in ways previous conglomerates never could.

Netflix iHeartMedia Deal

Netflix's determination to transform Netflix into a destination for all kinds of entertainment.

Netflix is plugging more podcasts into its programming lineup through an exclusive video podcast deal with iHeartMedia. Under the pact, video versions of more than 15 iHeartMedia original podcasts will stream in only on Netflix worldwide. With iHeartMedia's video-exclusive Netflix deal, video versions of the full podcast episodes will no longer be distributed on YouTube. This deal comes in addition to a similar deal reached by Netflix and Spotify to stream a collection of original podcasts on Netflix worldwide.

The issue?

The medium of podcasts has traditionally been associated with their open nature. Anyone can listen to it, at any time, at any place. Podcasts have become a way for people to build communities and connections through shared interests and interactions. Unlike YouTube, Netflix does not support comments, preventing audience interaction and reducing the "social" aspect of consuming podcasts. Netflix is reducing accessibility by making listeners pay for content that was once free.

Netflix and World Baseball Classic

Netflix's secured rights to stream World Baseball Classic in Japan and disruption in cultural viewing habits.

In August 2025, MLB announced the partnership with Netflix for exclusive broadcast rights in Japan. This means all Japanese baseball games would be moved to Netflix and exlusively available on the streaming platform.In this deal, Netflix aims to bring the games to a global audience and attract Japanese viewers.

  • This sport has proven to be popular among Japanese people, with reports stating that nearly 100 million of the 125 million people in the country watched at least some of the WBC in 2023.

The problem?

Individuals here in the U.S. are accustomed to sports being behind a paywall and having to pay multiple subscriptions to access content. However, Japanese fans are used to watching their national team's games on free-to-air national outlets. Most people are not used to Netflix. In Japan, television is free for a long time. Their preference for national content like Anime and variety programs has kept their broadcast TV alive. Some people have introduced subscriptions into their lives, but older people have not. With aims to change cultural viewing habits, Netflix's exclusivity pushed millions of Japanese people out of a game.

In a study conducted by The Sports Management Research Institute at Sanno University to 10,000 people, both men and women between the ages of 20 and 60 years old revealed

  • 4.9 percent of respondents said they signed up for Netflix because of the WBC
  • 8.8 percent said they'd consider signing up.
  • 68 percent said they had no plans to sign up
  • 17.3 percent said they'd already had a Netflix account.

    How Would a Netflix-WBD Merger Reshape the Entertainment Industry?

    According to analysts by Forbes, a merged Netflix-WBD could control between 30-40% of the U.S. streaming market at closing.

    Adding WBD's premium catalog and HBO Max subscribers would increase Netflix's United States market share and its overall global influence, creating a streaming powerhouse with unmatched subscriber scale, studio assets and global distribution. In addition, the acquisition of WBD would enhance Netflix's standing in the premium content category and add a library of highly recognizable franchises and IP, such as Harry Potter, Game of Thrones, The Lord of the Rings, The Matrix, and DC Studios staples such as Batman and Superman. Netflix could dictate licensing terms, shrink theatrical windows, and command premium deals for talent and distribution.
