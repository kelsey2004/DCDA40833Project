Literature Review

Across industry analysis and scholarly research, Netflix's rise is tied from its ability to leverage exclusive rights, global distribution, and algorithmic personalization to reshape entertainment ecosystems. Giving an overview, Holt's (2011) introduction provides detail of media empires and the long history of media regulation and deregulation. It goes over The Sherman Anti-Trust Act and what happened when President Regan pushed back against the law.

Sources such as Cowan (2025) and Martin (2025) position Netflix as a platform whose economic scale and acquisition strategies could be framed as a potential monopoly. Cowan analyzes Netflix's rise to becoming a streaming giant and the strategies Netflix took to differentiate itself from its competition. Martin explains the Warner Bros. Discovery and Netflix potential merging and its impact on the entertainment industry if the deal were to go through, stating it would reorder Hollywood's balance of power. While others like Blum (2026) and Spangler (2025) discuss Netflix's shift to live sporting events and the impact on national markets and Japanese cultural habits. Additionally, Netflix's expansion into video podcasts showcases their determination to transform the platform into a destination for all kinds of entertainment.

King (2022) discusses how Netflix disrupted the film industry norms by outbidding film studios on festival films, removing theatrical distribution, and limiting a film's visibility on their platform. The Academy Awards database provides proof of Netflix’s attempt to position itself as prestige through the growing number in nominations and wins recorded. All together, these works highlight Netflix's structural power to redefine access, visibility, and competition. Frater (2024) and Lobato (2019) focus on Netflix's international strategies. They discuss and demonstrate how Netflix enters a foreign market, commissioning local content, and reshaping regional media norms. These two sources highlight the growing tension between Netflix's strive for global reach and ability to outspend local competitors, raising concerns about Netflix's operating as a sort-of cultural imperialist. Quico (2019) takes note of the algorithmic infrastructure as a defining feature that has led to Netflix's rise. Their extensive user-behavior data has allowed for personalized homepage and tailor recommendations to create an individualized platform.
